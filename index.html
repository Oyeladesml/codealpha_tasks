The Mechanics of Deception

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of social engineering where attackers masquerade as trusted entities to steal credentials, data, or deliver malware. It exploits human psychology rather than software vulnerabilities.

The Hook Strategy

Most attacks begin with a "Hook"—a message designed to trigger an emotional response like urgency, fear, or curiosity—forcing the target to act impulsively.

Expanding Attack Vectors

Smishing

SMS-based phishing. Often uses fake package tracking alerts or urgent bank security warnings.

Vishing

Voice phishing. Attackers use phone spoofing or AI deepfake voice clones to bypass security controls.

Quishing

QR Code phishing. Exploits physical or digital QR codes to silently redirect users to credential-harvesting pages.

Financial & Operational Impact

  • Initial Entry: Phishing remains the #1 primary vector for enterprise malware delivery.
  • Dwell Time: Credential breaches take an average of over 200 days to detect.
  • Ransomware: A single clicked link can lead to complete network domain compromise.

The SLAM Inspection Framework

  • Sender: Verify the return path header. Check for look-alike domains or typosquatting.
  • Links: Hover before clicking. Inspect the absolute destination URL for hidden subdomains.
  • Attachments: Beware of unsolicited invoices, macro-enabled docs, or dangerous extensions (.zip, .exe).
  • Message: Look for psychological triggers—extreme urgency, requests to bypass protocols, or atypical formatting.

Genuine vs. Fraudulent Requests

Characteristic Legitimate Request Phishing Attempt
Sense of Urgency Action-oriented but adheres to corporate timelines. Panic-inducing, demanding immediate bypass of security guidelines.
Personalization Contains contextual, verifiable reference markers. Overly generic greetings or uncanny, AI-generated context.
Call to Action Directs to official internal systems or standard portals. Demands interaction via external links or obscure attachment methods.

The 5-Step Incident Protocol

1
STOP

Do not click links, download files, or reply.

2
VERIFY

Analyze headers and URLs using internal playbooks.

3
REPORT

Forward the artifact to the Security Operations Center.

4
ISOLATE

If clicked, immediately sever network connectivity.

5
REMEDIATE

Rotate exposed credentials and check audit logs.
